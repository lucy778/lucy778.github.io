How to choose an eyelash curler which works for your eye shape and doesn't pinch your eyelid

AKA this is probably the nerdiest beauty related reddit post I have seen

 Posted on February 1, 2020  |  6 minutes  |  1226 words  |  Lucy Rebekah  |  

There is a very cool post on Reddit which talks about how you can measure the dimensions of eyelash curlers and your eye to ensure they match up. It’s based on this nerdy and awesome blog post by Nikki Gilland (also see her more recent update here).

This is pretty important since if you’ve used a curler you will know that the wrong sized eyelash curler can pinch your eyes. And then if you hold it so that it doesn’t pinch, you won’t be able to curl the lashes close to their base for maximum effect and the lashes at the edges won’t be curled properly.

What do the measurements mean?

Here is a diagram showing what the measurements mean:

Chord

So bigger eyes and bigger/wider lash curlers will have a bigger chord. Although, since no one’s lashes extend all the way to the inner and outer corner of their eyes, I don’t think the chord being too small would be a common issue for people. On the flipside, if the lash curlers are too wide and bulky, they may dig into your nose when you are trying to curl your lashes.

Radius

The rounder the eye or the lash curler, the smaller the radius of the arc. The flatter the eye or curler, the larger the radius. If your eye and the curler curvature don’t match, you don’t get a good curl on all your lashes, and you can pinch your eyelid with the lash curler.

For me, if I hold the curler centred on my eye, it can pinch if it’s especially close to the lash line. I think this might be because the inner corner of my eye is further forward than the outer corner, and my lash curler is a little too rounded for my eye. So the part of my eyelid near my nose gets pinched. 😭 😭

Measuring your eyes and eyelash curler

Measuring the eyelash curler is pretty straightforward and Nikki explains it in her blog. You just clamp the lashes onto a piece of paper, trace the curve, and measure the dimensions.

Measuring eyes is a bit harder. You can use a string or piece of paper. The kind redditor explains how to do it with paper in this mini tutorial here. For me, it is a bit difficult to measure the chord length part, because my bottom eyelid bulges out, which means I’m not really measuring a straight line distance. I get around 33mm. I can look at myself straight on and measure the distance this way, but this isn’t accurate either, since my outer corner is set deeper in my face than my inner corner. I get 30mm this way. Ultimately I settled on 31mm after squishing paper flat on my face.

The second difficulty I ran into is working out the height. The tutorial gets you to measure the base and the arc length, mark them on paper, and tape them to replicate the arc of your eye. Then you trace and measure the height. I kind of struggled with this, and I don’t have tape. I don’t think it is necessary as you can just measure the length of the curved arc segment and plug it into an arc calculator instead. My chord length (“width of arc”) was 31mm, the “length of arc” was 36mm, which gave a radius of curvature of 19mm and height of 8mm.

Based on this, the closest match on Nikki’s list is actually the newer Kevyn Aucoin lash curler, and it would actually match my eye better than my current Kevyn Aucoin lash curler 😂 .

The data

I only actually have one lash curler I can measure myself. This is the Kevyn Aucoin lash curler. I got 31mm and 10mm vs. 32mm and 10mm in the Reddit post. Close enough, looks like the data is reasonably accurate. The unfortunate thing, though, is that brands change their eyelash curler design. If you look at Nikki’s more recent 2018 post, the Kevyn Aucoin lash curler design seems to have changed to be flatter and smaller. My curler is ancient, so it’s definitely the earlier design.

The table below has all the data from both the reddit post and the blog post. Don’t let the decimal points give you a false sense of precision or accuracy. Would guess there is +/-1mm error. Also keep in mind, there are other things to consider in your eyelash curler choice. For instance, in her second blog post, Nikki also includes information like the height of the eyelash curler gap, and how firm the pad is. If you wear falsies a lot you would want a big gap to fit them in there. And I personally prefer the pad to be firm so I don’t have to replace it frequently.
